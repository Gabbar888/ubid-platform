Submission summary. We propose a federated entity-resolution and activity-inference platform that
sits alongside Karnataka’s 40+ State department systems without modifying any of them. The platform
produces a hierarchical Unified Business Identifier (UBID) anchored to PAN where available, classifies

recalibrates the linkage models.

1 Problem Understanding
Karnataka Commerce & Industries cannot today answer first-order questions about its own industrial base
— how many businesses are operating, in what sectors, where, and with what recent activity — because
the State’s regulatory data is fragmented across 40+ department systems, each built in isolation with its
own schema, identifiers, and validation rules. The same business exists as different records in different
databases; central anchors like PAN and GSTIN are only partially captured; activity data sits trapped inside
each department system. Migrating to a unified database is not realistic at this scale, and the source systems
cannot be modified. The platform must therefore be additive: a federation layer that joins what exists rather
than replaces it.
We read the problem as two coupled sub-problems with an asymmetric structure.
Part A is an entity resolution problem on dirty, federated master data. Multiple independent
systems hold rows describing business entities, none designed to interoperate, and we must determine
which rows across systems refer to the same real-world business and assign each business a single identifier.
Part B is a state inference problem on an event stream that is not natively keyed by our identifier.
Once UBIDs exist, events arriving from department systems use their own record IDs; we must project
events onto UBIDs, and from the resulting per-UBID timeline infer a latent operational state.
These problems are sequenced — B is meaningless without A — but they are also mutually informing:
a UBID that never appears in any event stream is suspicious, and reviewer corrections to A change the
evidence base for B.
1.1 The constraints, read carefully
Each non-negotiable in the brief carries operational consequences that shape the architecture.
No source-system changes.
Linkage cannot be a foreign key written back into department databases. The UBID layer must be an
external graph that consumes pull-based or stream-subscription ingest. Schema discovery is our problem;
canonicalization happens in our layer, never theirs.
Synthetic / scrambled data only in the sandbox.
Our matching cannot rely on memorising real entities. Models must generalise from structural patterns,

Every decision explainable and reversible.
Pure black-box embedding-based matching cannot be the deciding layer. Embeddings (or any opaque

Wrong merge > missed merge in cost.
False positives compound: once two distinct businesses share a UBID, all their events co-mingle and the
verdict for both is corrupted. This is a precision-over-recall preference and pushes auto-link thresholds
conservative, with the cost of a longer reviewer queue.
1.2 Granularity: a hierarchy, not a single ID
A central design question is what a UBID actually identifies. Three reasonable definitions exist: a legal
entity (one PAN, one company), an establishment (a specific premise), or an operating unit (an establishment
plus its workforce / license footprint). Different department systems naturally key on different definitions:
GST registers legal entities (state-scoped); Factories Act registers establishments; KSPCB consents are
per-premise; Shop & Establishment registrations are per-branch. Picking one wrong definition breaks the
whole system.
Design choice
We adopt a three-layer hierarchy: a Legal Entity layer anchored to PAN; a UBID layer at the
establishment level (one premise, one operational unit, one UBID); and a Source Records layer
linking each department’s row to the UBID. Connection-level identifiers (BESCOM RR numbers,
water connections) attach as a fourth, infrastructure-keyed layer because they often do not carry PAN
and must be matched through address.
This mirrors how the source data is actually organised. It handles the proprietorship case correctly —
a single PAN with multiple unrelated establishments stays as separate UBIDs under one Legal Entity
— and lets us anchor multi-state operations cleanly: a Mumbai HQ with a Bangalore branch shares a
Legal Entity node, but only the Bangalore establishment receives a Karnataka UBID.
1.3 What success looks like in queries
The platform succeeds when Karnataka Commerce & Industries can run a query like “active factories in
pin code 560058 with no inspection in the last 18 months” against a single UBID-keyed warehouse and
receive an answer with the supporting evidence. Today this query is impossible because (i) “factories” is
locked inside the FBIS database, (ii) “inspection” events are scattered across multiple sources, and (iii)
there is no join key to bring them together. Our deliverable is the join key, the evidence, and the verdict.

2.1 The PAN and GSTIN anchors, used carefully
PAN is a 10-character code where the 4th character encodes entity type — P for individual, C for company,
F for partnership firm, H for HUF, T for trust, and so on. This is a free, structural signal: when we see a
PAN with 4th character P, the holder is a natural person, and we must not collapse multiple establishments
owned by that person into one business — they share a Legal Entity parent (the proprietor) but remain
distinct UBIDs.
GSTIN is a 15-character code structured as [state:2][PAN:10][entity:1][Z][checksum:1].
The state code is national but the embedded PAN is the same across states, so we extract digits 3–12 to
recover the legal-entity anchor for cross-state operations. The 13th digit lets a single legal entity hold
multiple GSTINs in the same state (different business verticals), which means GSTIN-to-UBID mapping
is many-to-one, not one-to-one. Our schema models this explicitly.
3 Part A: Entity Resolution
The goal of Part A is to produce, for each real-world business establishment, a single UBID that links
its records across source systems — with confidence-tagged evidence, conservative auto-merging, and a
reviewer queue for ambiguity. Our approach is a four-stage pipeline: canonicalize, block, score, cluster,
with explainability built into every stage.
3.1 Stage 1: Canonicalization
Each source system feeds an adapter that projects its records into a common internal schema. Adapters
perform deterministic, source-specific cleaning without touching the original data. The canonical record

carries: source identifier, original record ID (preserved verbatim for traceability), normalised name,
normalised address with extracted components, structural identifiers (PAN, GSTIN, derived legal-entity
PAN from GSTIN), sector category, contact fields, and ingestion metadata.
Name normalisation.
A deterministic pipeline strips legal-form noise (“Pvt Ltd”, “M/s”, “Private
Limited”, “LLP”, “& Sons”, “& Co”), expands common abbreviations (“Trdrs” →“Traders”), nor-
malises case and whitespace, and produces a token list. Kannada-script names are transliterated using
indic-transliteration (open source, runs locally) into a Roman canonical form so that “Sharma
Traders” and its Kannada equivalent block together. We deliberately do not use neural embeddings
for name matching: Bengaluru-specific business vocabulary (locality names, sector slang, family-name
compounds) is poorly represented in pretrained multilingual models, the marginal lift is small, and the
explainability cost is high. Probabilistic string distances (Jaro-Winkler on tokens, token-set ratio, Jaccard
on character n-grams) give us decomposable, defensible features.
Address canonicalization.
This is a first-class subsystem, not a post-processing step. The pipeline:
1. Pin code extraction via regex. Pin code is the strongest stable signal in an Indian address and partitions
the search space cleanly. It is the primary blocking key.
2. Component parsing via a hand-built grammar tuned for Bengaluru patterns: door numbers, cross/main
numbering (with ordinal ↔Roman conversion), stage / phase / block markers.
3. Locality normalisation via a curated synonym dictionary. “Peenya 2nd Stage”, “Peenya II Stage”,
“Peenya Industrial Area Phase 2” all collapse to a canonical key. The dictionary is bootstrapped from
KSPCB’s structured Industrial-Area / Taluk / District fields, then extended via reviewer feedback.
4. Optional geocoding for the lat/lng signal. We propose self-hosted Nominatim (OpenStreetMap) for the
production path; quality is patchy in India but the data never leaves our environment. As a sandbox-only
enrichment, an India-resident geocoder may be considered.
Design choice
We deliberately rule out hosted address-validation services (e.g. Google Address Validation API)
for the production path, despite their convenience. Three reasons: (i) it ships State PII to a third-
party cloud, conflicting with the data-handling non-negotiable; (ii) Indian coverage is in pre-GA
preview with no version-compatibility guarantee; and (iii) standard Maps Platform terms restrict
long-term storage of canonicalized output, which is incompatible with a persistent UBID layer. An
in-house deterministic parser plus a curated locality dictionary is more work upfront but is the only
architecturally defensible choice for a State-government data system.
3.2 Stage 2: Blocking
All-pairs comparison across millions of records is computationally intractable (∼1012 comparisons).
Blocking reduces this to candidate pairs without losing true matches. We use multiple blocking keys with
union, so a pair is a candidate if it shares any key:
• PAN equality (when both records have PAN) — highest precision block.
• Derived legal-entity PAN equality (extracted from GSTIN) — catches multi-vertical and multi-state
cases.
• Pin code + first 4 chars of normalised name (Soundex) — the workhorse block.
• Pin code + door number prefix — catches name-mismatch but same-premise cases.
• Phone number — secondary, since phones are reused across businesses.

3.3 Stage 3: Pairwise Scoring
For each candidate pair we compute a feature vector and a calibrated match probability. The scoring layer
is two-tiered: a deterministic tier that auto-decides high-confidence cases by rule, and a probabilistic tier
for everything else.
Deterministic tier.
If two records share a valid, non-null PAN and their other strong fields (pin code,
normalised name top-2 tokens) are not in active conflict, we accept the pair as a deterministic match
(probability ≈1). Symmetrically, if two records have different non-null PANs, we reject the pair as
a deterministic non-match. PAN equality with one record having PAN 4th-char P (proprietorship) is
downgraded to soft match: same legal owner, possibly different establishments, route to address comparison.
These deterministic rules cover an estimated 30–50% of cases at high precision and bypass the model
entirely. They are the easiest to explain (“these two records share PAN ABCDE1234F and pin code 560058”).
Probabilistic tier.
For the remaining pairs we compute a feature vector with ∼25 hand-crafted features:
• Name features: Jaro-Winkler on full normalised name; token-set ratio; Jaccard on character 3-grams;
longest common substring length; legal-form-stripped exact match indicator.
• Address features: pin code equality; door-number equality; locality canonical-key match; cross/main
numeric distance; Levenshtein on residual address tokens; geographic distance (when geocoded).
• Identifier features: GSTIN equality; phone equality; email domain match; partial-PAN agreement (for
cases where one side has PAN and the other has GSTIN).
• Structural features: sector-category compatibility; size-class compatibility (employee count, sanc-
tioned load); registration-date proximity.
Design choice
We use LightGBM for pairwise scoring, calibrated post-hoc with isotonic regression. Reasons: (i)
gradient-boosted trees handle our mix of continuous, binary, and categorical features natively; (ii)
per-feature SHAP values give us the explainability the brief demands — every match carries “these
features pushed the score up, these pushed it down”; (iii) LightGBM is fast at inference, which matters
at scale; (iv) it trains well on small labelled sets, which we will have in the synthetic-data sandbox.
We considered Fellegi-Sunter probabilistic record linkage (the classical approach). It is more in-
terpretable in its raw form but makes strong feature-independence assumptions that break for our
correlated features (name and address signals are not independent). LightGBM gives us better
calibration and recall at the cost of slightly more training infrastructure — a trade we accept.
3.4 Stage 4: Confidence Calibration and Thresholds
A raw model score is not a probability. A score of 0.85 must actually mean that 85% of pairs scored 0.85 are
true matches; otherwise our auto-link / review / reject thresholds are arbitrary. We calibrate using isotonic
regression on a held-out validation set, which is non-parametric and handles the non-linear miscalibration
typical of boosted trees. We monitor calibration drift via reliability diagrams refreshed weekly.
Threshold setting.
Given the calibrated probability p and the brief’s directive that wrong merges cost
more than missed merges, we set:

• p ≥0.95: auto-link. At this threshold, expected precision is high enough that the false-merge cost is

• 0.55 ≤p < 0.95: review queue. Routed to a human reviewer with the feature breakdown.

These thresholds are not hard-coded constants; they are tuned on the synthetic ground truth in Round 2.
The auto-link threshold is the single most important operating parameter, and we expose it as a runtime
configuration so that Karnataka C&I can tighten or loosen it based on reviewer capacity.
3.5 Stage 5: Clustering Across Multiple Pairs
Pairwise scores produce edges in a graph. Naive transitive closure is dangerous — if A-B and B-C are both
0.95 but A-C is 0.40, blindly merging {A,B,C} would be wrong. We use correlation clustering with a
positive/negative edge formulation: edges with p ≥0.95 are positive, p < 0.20 are negative, the middle
range is unobserved. The clustering objective minimises disagreements (positive edges across clusters plus
negative edges within clusters).
Design choice
For tractability at scale we use a greedy correlation-clustering heuristic with conflict-aware merging:
candidate merges are evaluated against existing cluster members and rejected if they would violate
a strong negative edge. This preserves the precision-over-recall preference and produces auditable
merge histories. For the Round 2 sandbox we additionally run networkx.connected_components
on positive-only edges as a sanity baseline.
Reversibility.
Cluster assignments are versioned. Every merge carries a timestamp, a contributing-edge
set, and a reviewer-id (or auto). A reviewer’s must-link or cannot-link decision becomes a constraint that
downstream clustering passes must respect. We never destructively rewrite source identifiers — the UBID
is a layer on top, queryable via a lookup service.
4 Part B: Activity Inference
Once UBIDs exist, events arrive from department systems keyed by their own record IDs. We project each
event onto a UBID via the linkage table; events that cannot be confidently joined go to a quarantine queue
(Section 4.6). The remaining UBID-keyed event timeline drives the activity verdict.
4.1 Why naive thresholding fails
The simplest approach — “any event in the last 90 days →Active” — breaks for several reasons. Different
events have different activity strength (an electricity payment is a continuity signal; a state-driven inspection
is not a business signal at all). Different events have different natural cadences (annual returns are once a
year; meter readings are monthly; not seeing each for 6 months means very different things). And dormancy
is a real distinct state, not a halfway score: a fireworks shop is genuinely dormant for nine months and
active for three.

4.3 From decayed signals to verdict
For each UBID we aggregate decayed contributions into a continuity score S = ∑T ∑e∈events(T) cT(∆te),
plus a vector of per-signal aggregates that preserve which sources contributed. The verdict is produced in
three stages:
1. Deterministic overrides first. If a formal closure event exists (S&E closure intimation, KSPCB consent
revoked, factory delicensed), the verdict is Closed regardless of score. If only a registration event exists
and the UBID is less than 90 days old, the verdict is Nascent.
2. Rule-bounded thresholding. The continuity score is mapped through learned thresholds tuned on
the synthetic ground truth. Default cuts: S ≥1.5 →Active, 0.4 ≤S < 1.5 →Dormant, S < 0.4 →
Closed-by-silence.
3. Sector-aware adjustment. Seasonal sectors (event halls, fireworks, agricultural processing) carry a
sector prior that widens the Dormant band and slows the Closed transition. The prior is loaded from a
sector configuration file, not learned — it must be auditable.

Design choice
We deliberately keep the activity engine rule-driven with tuned parameters, not end-to-end learned.
A learned classifier (HMM, sequence model) would be more powerful but harder to explain and harder
to debug when a verdict surprises a department. A rule engine where every parameter has a name
and a justification is what survives a procurement review. The tuning process can be supervised —
reviewers correcting verdicts produce labels — but the model remains a transparent function.
4.4 Explainability of the verdict
Every verdict is shipped with an evidence timeline: the events that contributed, their decayed weights, the
deterministic rules that fired, and the threshold band. A reviewer (or department officer) sees: “Closed
because no electricity bill paid in 14 months, no Form 20 filed in 2024, KSPCB consent expired 2023-08,
no overriding renewal event detected.” The same data is exposed via API so that a department’s own
dashboards can surface it.
4.5 Edge cases the activity engine handles by design
• Seasonal businesses. Sector prior widens Dormant band. A wedding-hall UBID with summer-only
events stays Dormant in winter, not Closed.
• Late filings burst. Three years of returns filed in one month boost the score, but each event’s ∆t is
computed from its filing date, not its period date, so a true catch-up is correctly weighted.
• Bill paid by absent owner. Single signal, low marginal contribution; if BESCOM payments are the
only activity, the UBID stays Dormant rather than auto-promoting to Active.
• Migration to a new establishment. If A and B are correctly linked as one UBID (Part A), events from
both contribute. If they are mistakenly separate, A goes Closed-by-silence and B becomes Nascent — a
pattern the reviewer queue surfaces for re-linkage (Section 5).
• Brand-new UBID with one event. Nascent state, hold-out period before any Closed transition.
4.6 Quarantine for unjoined events
Events arrive keyed by source-system identifiers. Most join cleanly to a UBID via the linkage table. Some
do not — the source row has not been ingested yet, the linkage was below auto-link threshold, or the event
refers to a sub-identifier (BESCOM RR number) not yet attached. These events go to a quarantine queue,
indexed by source identifier, and re-played whenever the linkage table is updated. We never silently drop
an unjoined event; the reviewer dashboard shows quarantine size as a top-level metric.
5 Human-in-the-Loop Review
The reviewer workflow is the system’s correctness backstop. It exists for two reasons: to handle ambiguous
matches the model cannot decide, and to feed labelled corrections back into the model. Both are first-class.
5.1 Triage: what the reviewer sees
The review queue is not a flat list. It is sorted by informativeness — cases where reviewer effort produces
the most learning. Informativeness combines:
• Uncertainty: pairs whose calibrated probability sits near 0.7–0.85 (high disagreement) are prioritised
over pairs near 0.55 (likely no-match).
• Impact: clusters whose merge would affect many downstream events get higher priority.
• Disagreement among signals: a pair where name match is high but address match is low is more
informative than a pair where everything weakly agrees.

5.2 What the reviewer does
The console presents a candidate pair (or candidate cluster) with:
• Side-by-side records with raw and canonicalized fields highlighted.
• Per-feature contribution from the model (SHAP-style “name pushed +0.18, address pushed −0.12”).
• Hits from the deterministic tier, if any.
• Existing must-link / cannot-link constraints affecting the cluster.
The reviewer decides: Confirm match, Reject (keep separate), Defer to senior, or Flag data quality
issue (e.g. source record looks corrupt). Decisions are timestamped and reviewer-attributed.
5.3 Two-tier reviewer structure
We propose a tiered structure to balance throughput and quality: junior reviewers for routine ambiguity
(5–10 min per case), senior reviewers for escalations and policy-setting cases (cross-state mergers, owner-
change disputes). A junior reviewer can defer to senior; a senior reviewer’s decision is binding and becomes
a precedent (added to the must-link / cannot-link constraint set).
5.4 Feedback loop: how decisions improve the system
1. Direct labels. Each reviewer decision is a labelled pair. Labels accumulate in a training table.
2. Periodic retraining. The pairwise model is retrained on a schedule (weekly initially, monthly at steady
state) and re-calibrated. New thresholds are A/B-tested against the previous model on a held-out set
before promotion.
3. Constraint propagation. Must-link and cannot-link decisions are stored as graph constraints and
respected by every subsequent clustering pass. A reviewer’s “these are different businesses” decision
survives even if the model’s score on the pair drifts upward.
4. Synonym dictionary updates. When a reviewer marks a match where two locality strings refer to the
same place, the canonical-locality dictionary gets a new entry, improving future blocking and matching.
5. Activity-engine corrections. A reviewer who flags an Active/Dormant/Closed verdict as wrong
produces a label that re-tunes the activity thresholds and per-signal weights.
6 Architecture
The platform sits as a federation layer between source department systems and Karnataka C&I’s analytical
workloads. Source systems remain untouched. We pull or stream-subscribe to their data; nothing flows the
other way.

6.2 Storage choices and why
Postgres for the canonicalized record store and the linkage table. ACID, mature, supports versioned writes
via temporal tables, and is the source of truth.
OpenSearch for blocking indices and fuzzy text search in the reviewer console. Inverted indices make
union-blocking fast; n-gram analysers handle approximate matching natively.
Neo4j for the entity graph. The hierarchy (Legal Entity →UBID →Source Records) plus must-link /
cannot-link constraints is fundamentally graph-shaped. Relational joins to traverse this would be painful;
Cypher queries are natural.
DuckDB on Parquet for the event warehouse. Append-only, columnar, fast for the analytical query
class. No need for a heavyweight warehouse at expected event volumes; DuckDB embeds in the analytics
service and reads Parquet directly from object storage.
Redis for the linkage hot-path. UBID lookups are read-heavy and latency-sensitive; Redis caches the
source-id →UBID map with TTL-based invalidation on linkage updates.
Kafka for event ingestion. Decouples source-adapter cadence from consumer cadence; quarantine is a
topic, not a special data path.

Trade-off
This stack is heavier than strictly necessary for an MVP. A simpler “Postgres for everything plus

6.3 Model choices and why
Pairwise scorer: LightGBM with isotonic calibration.
Justified in Section 3. The single most important learned component.
Activity engine: rule-driven with tuned parameters.
Justified in Section 4. We deliberately do not use a sequence model here.
Name matching: probabilistic string distances, no embeddings.
Bengaluru-specific business vocabulary (locality names, sector terms, family-name compounds) is
poorly represented in pretrained multilingual models. The lift from embeddings is small, training
is unstable on small synthetic data, and the explainability cost is high. We commit to Jaro-Winkler,
token-set ratio, and character n-gram Jaccard as the name-comparison primitives.
Address normalisation: deterministic parser plus curated dictionary.
Avoids hosted APIs (data-residency), avoids neural parsing (explainability), and is bootstrappable from
KSPCB’s clean structured fields.
Transliteration: indic-transliteration (open source, local).
Kannada ↔Roman conversion needs to be deterministic and on-prem.
6.4 Data flow
A new source-system snapshot arrives. The adapter projects it to the canonical schema. The canonicalization
service normalises names and addresses. New / changed records are indexed in OpenSearch. The pairwise
scorer fetches candidate pairs by union-block, computes features, and emits scored pairs. The clustering
service consumes scored pairs, applies constraints, and updates the UBID graph in Neo4j; the linkage table
in Postgres is updated atomically; Redis cache is invalidated for affected source identifiers. Any pair in the
review band emits a record to the reviewer queue. Reviewer decisions write back to the constraint set and
the labelled-pair table. Independently, event topics fan in from source systems; events are joined to UBIDs
through Redis (Postgres on miss); joined events append to the DuckDB store; unjoined events queue for
replay. The activity engine reads the event store on a rolling window and produces verdicts that materialise
into a per-UBID status table queried by the API.
7 Respecting the Non-Negotiables
Each constraint from the brief shapes a concrete part of the system. We enumerate them with the
corresponding architectural responses.
No source-system changes.
The platform is strictly downstream. Source-system adapters consume
snapshots, change-data-capture streams, or scheduled exports — whichever each system supports today.
No adapter writes back to a source system. The UBID is held in our graph; source records keep their
original identifiers, and we map between the two via the linkage table. If a source system adds a new field
tomorrow, only its adapter changes; the canonical schema absorbs the difference.

Synthetic / scrambled data only.
Every learned component (pairwise scorer, isotonic calibrator, activity-
engine parameter tuning) is trained and validated on synthetic ground truth in the Round 2 sandbox. We do
not bake real-data assumptions into models. The deterministic tier (PAN/GSTIN equality rules, address
grammar) is structural, not data-dependent, and works equally on real or scrambled inputs. We make zero
hosted-LLM calls, and we do not send data to third-party APIs in the production path; address validation is
in-house, transliteration is in-house, geocoding (where used) is self-hosted Nominatim.
Explainable and reversible decisions.
Every linkage carries a versioned evidence record: which features
contributed, which deterministic rules fired, which reviewer (or auto) made the call. The UBID graph
stores merges as discrete events with full lineage; an unmerge is a first-class operation that propagates to
the linkage table, the event-quarantine queue, and downstream verdicts. Activity verdicts are produced by
a transparent rule chain with named parameters; the verdict API returns the evidence timeline alongside the
label.
Wrong merge > missed merge.
The auto-link threshold is conservative (p ≥0.95). The deterministic
non-match rule (different non-null PANs) is a hard reject. Clustering is correlation-clustering with conflict
resolution rather than transitive closure, so weak chains do not collapse distinct entities. Reviewer-issued
cannot-link constraints are durable: the model can never auto-merge a pair that a reviewer has rejected.
8 Risks, Trade-offs, and How We Handle Them
8.1 Technical risks
Calibration drift.
As source data evolves, the relationship between model score and true match probability can drift.
Mitigation: reliability diagrams refreshed weekly, automated alerts when calibration error exceeds a
threshold, scheduled recalibration on the latest reviewer-labelled set.
Address normalisation gaps.
Our locality dictionary will miss long-tail areas, especially outside Bengaluru. Mitigation: reviewer
feedback captures every locality-pair the system gets wrong, dictionary grows monotonically. Initial
coverage focused on Bengaluru Urban (the brief’s sample area) and expanded as new districts are
ingested.
BESCOM data quality.
Connection-holder names are often individuals, not businesses; internal identifier inconsistency is
documented. Mitigation: BESCOM connections cluster among themselves first (RR ↔Account
ID ↔Consumer ID), then attach to a UBID via address only, with the auto-link threshold raised.
BESCOM-only UBIDs (no S&E / Factories / KSPCB linkage) are flagged for reviewer attention before
being committed.
Quarantine queue growth.
If a large source system is delayed, unjoined events pile up. Mitigation: quarantine size is a top-level
dashboard metric; replay is automatic on linkage updates; aged quarantine items (> 90 days) generate
alerts.
Reviewer bottleneck.
If auto-link threshold is too conservative, reviewer queue grows unboundedly. Mitigation: threshold is
a runtime parameter; queue length is monitored; an active-learning ordering keeps marginal reviewer
effort high-value even under load.

8.2 Operational and political risks
Source-system access friction.
Departments may resist exposing data, even read-only. Mitigation: ingestion design is flexible (SFTP
drops, snapshot APIs, message queues); legal/data-sharing agreement happens before adapter imple-
mentation; pilot with the 3–4 most cooperative departments first.
Schema drift in source systems.
A department changes its schema without notice. Mitigation: adapters validate canonical-schema
conformance on every batch and fail loudly rather than silently corrupting data. Alerts go to the
platform team and the source-system owner.
Definitional disputes.
Two departments may disagree about whether a closed business was Dormant or Closed at a given
point. Mitigation: the activity engine produces evidence, not just a label. The label is auditable and
overrideable; departments retain authority over their own data.
8.3 Trade-offs we made deliberately
Trade-off
Precision over recall in linkage. We set p ≥0.95 for auto-link and route the 0.55–0.95 band to
humans. This is a deliberate cost-sensitive choice (wrong merges > missed merges) at the price of a
larger reviewer workload. We do not regret this.
Trade-off
Rule-driven activity engine over a learned sequence model. A learned model would likely score
higher on a benchmark; a rule engine survives a procurement review. We chose the rule engine.
Trade-off
No neural embeddings in the matching path. We forgo the marginal lift in name-similarity recall in
exchange for explainability and stability under synthetic data. If, in Round 2, the LightGBM scorer’s
recall on the genuine ambiguous tail falls short, we revisit this — but only with a self-hosted Indic
model and only as one feature, never as the deciding layer.
Trade-off
Heavier storage stack vs. MVP simplicity. A Postgres-only MVP would ship faster but caps the
system at small scale. We choose the complete stack with substitutes (DuckDB, NetworkX in-memory,
Postgres-only blocking) acceptable for the Round 2 prototype.

11 Closing Statement
What we are proposing is not exotic. The components are well-understood — federated ingestion, probabilistic
record linkage, calibrated classification, rule-driven state inference, active-learning review — and the architectural
choices favour boring correctness over novelty. The hard work in this problem is not invention; it is the disciplined
application of the right primitives to a domain whose data is messy, whose constraints are real, and whose stakes (a
wrong merge corrupts every event for two businesses) are unforgiving.
We have made every design choice with three things in mind: that the platform must sit alongside source systems
without disturbing them, that every decision must be readable by a human, and that wrong merges are worse than
missed merges. The architecture, the model choices, the threshold settings, and the reviewer workflow all fall out of
these three commitments.
Karnataka Commerce & Industries should, when this platform is operating, be able to ask its industrial base
questions that are impossible today — and receive answers backed by evidence it can verify. That is the deliverable.
